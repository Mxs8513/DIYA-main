CHEM 1301 — General Chemistry I
Course Notes: Key Concepts and Reference Material

=== CHAPTER 1: ATOMIC STRUCTURE AND THE PERIODIC TABLE ===

Atomic Structure:
- Atoms consist of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in orbitals.
- Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
- Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
- Electron configuration follows the Aufbau principle: fill lowest energy orbitals first.
  Example: Sodium (Na, Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹

Periodic Trends:
- Atomic radius decreases left to right across a period (increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer).
- Atomic radius increases top to bottom in a group (additional electron shells).
- Ionization energy increases left to right; decreases top to bottom.
- Electronegativity: Fluorine (4.0) is the most electronegative element.

=== CHAPTER 2: CHEMICAL BONDING ===

Ionic Bonds:
- Form between metals and nonmetals through electron transfer.
- The metal loses electrons (becomes cation, positive charge) and the nonmetal gains electrons (becomes anion, negative charge).
- Example: NaCl — sodium gives one electron to chlorine.
  Na → Na⁺ + e⁻  |  Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻  |  Combined: Na⁺Cl⁻
- Ionic compounds form crystal lattice structures with high melting points (NaCl melts at 801°C).
- Ionic compounds conduct electricity when dissolved in water (electrolytes).

Covalent Bonds:
- Form between two nonmetals through electron sharing.
- Single bond: one shared pair (e.g., H–H in H₂). Bond order = 1.
- Double bond: two shared pairs (e.g., O=O in O₂). Bond order = 2.
- Triple bond: three shared pairs (e.g., N≡N in N₂). Bond order = 3.
- Polar covalent: unequal sharing when atoms have different electronegativities (e.g., H–Cl).
- Nonpolar covalent: equal sharing (e.g., H–H, Cl–Cl).
- Rule of thumb: ΔEN < 0.4 = nonpolar covalent; 0.4–1.7 = polar covalent; > 1.7 = ionic.

VSEPR Theory (Molecular Geometry):
- Electron pairs repel each other and adopt geometry that minimizes repulsion.
- 2 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs → linear (180°), e.g., CO₂
- 3 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs → trigonal planar (120°), e.g., BF₃
- 4 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs → tetrahedral (109.5°), e.g., CH₄
- 4 pairs total, 1 lone pair → trigonal pyramidal (~107°), e.g., NH₃
- 4 pairs total, 2 lone pairs → bent (~104.5°), e.g., H₂O

=== CHAPTER 3: STOICHIOMETRY AND MOLAR MASS ===

The Mole Concept:
- 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number).
- Molar mass (g/mol) = mass in grams of one mole of a substance.
- Molar mass = sum of atomic masses of all atoms in formula.

Calculating Molar Mass — Examples:
- H₂O: (2 × 1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 g/mol
- NaCl: 22.990 + 35.453 = 58.443 g/mol
- H₂SO₄: (2 × 1.008) + 32.06 + (4 × 16.00) = 98.076 g/mol
- C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose): (6 × 12.011) + (12 × 1.008) + (6 × 16.00) = 180.156 g/mol

Mole Conversions:
- moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol)
- mass (g) = moles × molar mass (g/mol)
- particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³

Percent Composition:
- % element = (mass of element in 1 mol / molar mass of compound) × 100

Empirical and Molecular Formulas:
- Empirical formula: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.
- Molecular formula: actual number of atoms (multiple of empirical formula).
- To find empirical formula from % composition: convert % to grams, then to moles, then find smallest ratio.

=== CHAPTER 4: BALANCING CHEMICAL EQUATIONS ===

Rules for Balancing:
1. Write the unbalanced equation with correct formulas.
2. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
3. Add coefficients (not subscripts) to balance.
4. Always balance by inspection or trial/error; never change subscripts.
5. Check: total atoms of each element must be equal on both sides.

Common Examples:
- Combustion of methane: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
- Formation of water: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
- Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂
- Synthesis of ammonia: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
- Iron rusting: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
- Neutralization: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Limiting Reagents:
- The limiting reagent is the reactant that runs out first, stopping the reaction.
- Excess reagent: the reactant left over after the reaction.
- To find limiting reagent: convert each reactant to moles of product. The reactant that gives FEWER moles of product is the limiting reagent.
- Theoretical yield: maximum amount of product possible from the limiting reagent.
- Percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%

Example: React 5.0 g H₂ with 32.0 g O₂ to form H₂O (2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O)
- Moles H₂ = 5.0 / 2.016 = 2.48 mol → can make 2.48 mol H₂O
- Moles O₂ = 32.0 / 32.00 = 1.00 mol → can make 2 × 1.00 = 2.00 mol H₂O
- O₂ gives fewer moles of product → O₂ is the limiting reagent
- Theoretical yield of H₂O = 2.00 mol × 18.016 g/mol = 36.0 g

=== CHAPTER 5: SOLUTIONS AND CONCENTRATION ===

Molarity (M):
- Molarity = moles of solute / liters of solution
- M = n / V(L)
- To prepare a solution: weigh out n = M × V grams of solute, dissolve in less than the final volume of solvent, then dilute to final volume.

Dilution:
- M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ (moles of solute stay constant)
- Example: dilute 25.0 mL of 6.0 M HCl to make 150.0 mL solution.
  M₂ = (6.0 × 25.0) / 150.0 = 1.0 M

Other Concentration Units:
- Molality (m) = moles solute / kg solvent (used for colligative properties)
- Mole fraction (χ) = moles component / total moles all components
- Weight percent = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100%

Solubility Rules (memorize these):
- All Group 1 (alkali metal) salts are soluble.
- All nitrate (NO₃⁻) and acetate (CH₃COO⁻) salts are soluble.
- All chlorides (Cl⁻) are soluble EXCEPT AgCl, PbCl₂, Hg₂Cl₂.
- All sulfates (SO₄²⁻) are soluble EXCEPT BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄.
- All carbonates (CO₃²⁻) are insoluble EXCEPT Group 1 and NH₄⁺.
- All hydroxides (OH⁻) are insoluble EXCEPT Group 1, Ba(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂.

=== CHAPTER 6: ACIDS, BASES, AND pH ===

Definitions:
- Arrhenius: acid produces H⁺ in water; base produces OH⁻ in water.
- Brønsted-Lowry: acid donates H⁺ (proton donor); base accepts H⁺ (proton acceptor).
- Lewis: acid accepts electron pair; base donates electron pair.

Strong Acids (memorize — fully dissociate in water):
HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄

Strong Bases (memorize):
NaOH, KOH, LiOH, Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂

pH Scale:
- pH = -log[H⁺] (concentration in mol/L)
- pH < 7: acidic | pH = 7: neutral | pH > 7: basic
- pOH = -log[OH⁻]
- pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C)
- Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C

Titration:
- Used to determine the concentration of an unknown acid or base.
- At the equivalence point: moles acid = moles base (for monoprotic).
- Equation: MₐVₐ = M_bV_b (for monoprotic acid + monoprotic base)
- Indicator: changes color near the equivalence point (e.g., phenolphthalein turns pink at ~pH 8.2–10).
- Titration curve: shows pH vs. volume of titrant added.

Example: 25.00 mL of HCl titrated with 0.1500 M NaOH, equivalence at 32.50 mL.
- moles NaOH = 0.03250 L × 0.1500 mol/L = 0.004875 mol
- moles HCl = moles NaOH = 0.004875 mol (1:1 ratio)
- [HCl] = 0.004875 mol / 0.02500 L = 0.1950 M

=== CHAPTER 7: THERMOCHEMISTRY ===

Key Terms:
- System: the part of the universe we study (e.g., the reaction).
- Surroundings: everything else.
- Exothermic reaction: releases heat to surroundings; ΔH < 0 (negative).
- Endothermic reaction: absorbs heat from surroundings; ΔH > 0 (positive).

Enthalpy (H):
- ΔH = H_products − H_reactants
- ΔH is a state function (independent of path).
- Hess's Law: if a reaction can be expressed as sum of other reactions, its ΔH = sum of their ΔH values.

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔHf°):
- ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔHf°(products) − Σ ΔHf°(reactants)
- ΔHf° of any pure element in its standard state = 0.

Specific Heat Capacity:
- q = mcΔT
  where q = heat (J), m = mass (g), c = specific heat (J/g·°C), ΔT = temperature change (°C)
- Specific heat of water = 4.184 J/g·°C
- Specific heat of ice = 2.09 J/g·°C; steam = 2.01 J/g·°C

Calorimetry:
- q_system = −q_surroundings (conservation of energy)
- Coffee-cup calorimeter: constant pressure, measures ΔH directly.
- Bomb calorimeter: constant volume, measures ΔE; ΔH ≈ ΔE for reactions with no Δn_gas.

=== CHAPTER 8: CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM ===

Equilibrium Constant (K):
- For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
  Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b
- Only aqueous (aq) and gaseous (g) species appear in K expression.
- Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted (activity = 1).

Interpreting K values:
- K >> 1: equilibrium lies to the right (mostly products).
- K << 1: equilibrium lies to the left (mostly reactants).
- K ≈ 1: significant amounts of both reactants and products at equilibrium.

Le Chatelier's Principle:
- If stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to relieve that stress.
- Adding reactant → shifts right (more products form).
- Adding product → shifts left (more reactants form).
- Removing a component → shifts toward that component.
- Increasing pressure (for gas-phase reactions) → shifts toward side with fewer moles of gas.
- Increasing temperature for endothermic reaction → shifts right (K increases).
- Increasing temperature for exothermic reaction → shifts left (K decreases).
- Adding catalyst → does NOT shift equilibrium; only speeds up reaching equilibrium.

Reaction Quotient (Q):
- Same expression as K but uses current (non-equilibrium) concentrations.
- If Q < K: reaction proceeds forward (toward products).
- If Q > K: reaction proceeds reverse (toward reactants).
- If Q = K: system is at equilibrium.

=== CHAPTER 9: GASES AND THE IDEAL GAS LAW ===

Ideal Gas Law:
- PV = nRT
  P = pressure (atm), V = volume (L), n = moles, R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K, T = temperature (K)
- Note: T must ALWAYS be in Kelvin. K = °C + 273.15

Gas Laws (special cases of PV=nRT):
- Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T and n)
- Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P and n)
- Gay-Lussac's Law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (constant V and n)
- Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP):
- T = 273.15 K (0°C), P = 1 atm
- 1 mole of ideal gas at STP occupies 22.414 L (molar volume)

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures:
- P_total = P_A + P_B + P_C + ...
- Each gas exerts pressure independently.
- P_A = χ_A × P_total (where χ is mole fraction)

Real vs. Ideal Gases:
- Ideal gas: particles have no volume and no intermolecular forces.
- Real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature.
- Van der Waals equation: (P + an²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT
  where a corrects for intermolecular attractions and b corrects for molecular volume.

=== SIGNIFICANT FIGURES ===

Rules:
1. All non-zero digits are significant.
2. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant (e.g., 1002 has 4 sig figs).
3. Leading zeros are NOT significant (e.g., 0.0052 has 2 sig figs).
4. Trailing zeros after decimal point ARE significant (e.g., 1.500 has 4 sig figs).
5. Trailing zeros without decimal point are ambiguous (use scientific notation to be clear).

Operations:
- Multiplication/division: answer has same sig figs as value with fewest sig figs.
- Addition/subtraction: answer has same decimal places as value with fewest decimal places.

=== EXAM TOPICS AND COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS ===

Common mistakes to avoid:
1. Forgetting to convert Celsius to Kelvin in gas law problems.
2. Using mass instead of moles in equilibrium expressions.
3. Including solids/liquids in the K expression.
4. Confusing molarity (mol/L solution) with molality (mol/kg solvent).
5. Adding subscripts instead of coefficients when balancing equations.
6. Forgetting that ΔHf° of elements in standard state = 0.
7. Confusing limiting reagent (based on moles of product) with the reactant present in smaller amount.
